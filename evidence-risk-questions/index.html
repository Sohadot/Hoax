Evidence-Risk Questions

Questions come before conclusions. This page gives you governed, manual prompts for reading synthetic media evidence — without upload, scoring, verdict labels, or automated judgment.

Why Questions Come Before Conclusions

Under pressure, people skip structure and reach for true/false shorthand. Evidence-risk questions restore sequence: artifact, source, chain, context, claim scope, boundary — then posture. Hoax.ai keeps that sequence public and usable.

Source Questions

  • Who originated this artifact?
  • Who published it first in this circulation path?
  • Is the named source the same as the claiming party?

Provenance Questions

  • Can custody or handling be described?
  • Are format and metadata consistent with stated origin?
  • Where does the chain go silent?

Context Questions

  • When and where was this artifact situated?
  • What surrounding information was removed in sharing?
  • Does the narrative fit temporal and situational constraints?

Traceability Questions

  • Can each repost be linked backward to a prior copy?
  • Is the claim anchored to a inspectable record?
  • What would break if one link in the chain failed?

Claim Drift Questions

  • What did the artifact show at origin versus what is claimed now?
  • Has subject accusation replaced artifact description?
  • Which words add moral urgency without adding evidence?

Boundary Questions

  • Am I describing the artifact or judging a connected subject?
  • Would my language still hold if the subject were unknown?
  • Am I using posture vocabulary or verdict vocabulary?

Posture Questions

  • What condition can be stated proportionately from current structure?
  • What remains not assessable yet?
  • What additional evidence would change posture — not prove guilt?

Escalation Questions

  • Who must see this before public action — and why?
  • What harm follows from acting on thin structure?
  • Is escalation driven by evidence condition or narrative urgency?

Questions That Are Unsafe to Answer Too Early

Avoid forcing answers to “Is this authentic?” or “Is this misleading?” before source, provenance, context, and traceability are examined. Those questions compress structure into verdict shape. Prefer: “What does the evidence layer support right now?”

How Hoax.ai Keeps the Boundary

Hoax.ai publishes manual utilities — checklists, maps, synthetic examples, and question flows — that preserve artifact-first discipline. No public classifier, no upload intake, no score output, no automated report.

Before You Share, Reject, Accuse, or Escalate, Ask

  • What is the artifact?
  • What is the claim?
  • Who is the source?
  • What context is missing?
  • What has changed between the artifact and the claim?
  • What cannot be concluded yet?

Non-Verdict Boundary

This question guide is a public utility. It does not verify truth, certify evidence, or accept file uploads. Classify the evidence artifact. Do not perform the verdict.
