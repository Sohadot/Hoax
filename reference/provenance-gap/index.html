Provenance Gap

A Provenance Gap is a missing or weakened link in the history, custody, origin, transfer, modification, or contextual chain of an evidence artifact—it does not prove manipulation, deception, or subject guilt.

Definition

Provenance Gap is the Hoax.ai term for a break, absence, or weakening in the chain that connects an artifact to its origin, custody history, transfer path, modification record, or supporting contextual documentation.

The gap is descriptive: it names what is missing or uncertain in chain context. It does not assign cause, intent, or culpability. A gap may appear when records were never created, when custody cannot be reconstructed, or when publication path obscures earlier handling.

Why It Matters

When chain context is incomplete, teams still face pressure to act, publish, or conclude. Without gap vocabulary, that pressure converts missing context into implied misconduct or assumed authenticity.

Provenance Gap gives institutions a disciplined way to say the chain cannot yet support confident structural description—without claiming manipulation is proven or that the artifact is therefore false.

Gap language also connects to synthetic fragility: when evidence can be manufactured or recombined, missing provenance is itself a structural signal that posture descriptions must narrow.

What It Can Describe

A Provenance Gap can describe missing origin documentation, unclear custody transfers, absent modification history, broken chain-of-record links, or contextual information too weak to support bounded claims about artifact handling.

It can explain why further examination may be required before publication or escalation. It can note when gap severity contributes to Limited Evidence posture or moves discussion toward Not Assessable when signals cannot support more.

What It Cannot Decide

A Provenance Gap cannot prove manipulation, tampering, deception, or inauthenticity. It cannot prove that an artifact was altered with intent. It cannot identify a guilty subject, responsible party, or fraudulent actor.

Gap language describes structural uncertainty in the chain—it does not replace forensic findings, legal process, or institutional investigation. Absence of provenance is not evidence of guilt.

Relationship to Evidence Posture

Provenance clarity is a core input to evidence posture. Where gaps dominate, posture descriptions must narrow: teams may describe what cannot be supported rather than overstating what is known.

Posture integrates gap severity with source confidence, contextual fit, and coherence signals without collapsing gap presence into verdict language.

Relationship to Artifact–Subject Separation

Provenance Gap describes artifact and chain condition—not subject conduct. Stating that custody history is missing does not accuse whoever held, transferred, or published the material.

Separation requires that gap language remain on material structure. It must not become shorthand for blaming a person, institution, or event connected to the artifact.

Common Misreadings

  • Misreading: A provenance gap proves the artifact was faked. Correction: It proves chain context is incomplete—not that fabrication occurred.
  • Misreading: No gap means the artifact is authentic. Correction: Documented chain support helps posture description; it does not certify truth.
  • Misreading: Gap language identifies who tampered. Correction: Gaps describe missing links, not assigned culpability.
