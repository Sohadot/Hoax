Definition

Interpretation risk is the chance that meaning is being added, compressed, distorted, or overextended beyond what the available evidence can support. It arises when context, source, audience, timing, or claim framing is unstable relative to the artifact.

Interpretation risk is analytical vocabulary for evidence conditions — not a misconduct finding, not a legal determination, and not a binary authenticity label.

The top-level route complements Interpretation Risk in the governed reference layer while serving Sprint 92 deep-reference retrieval for humans and AI agents.

Why It Matters

Most harmful public errors are interpretive errors: the artifact is visible, but the meaning attached to it outruns support. Interpretation risk gives reviewers a name for that condition without jumping to intent or falsehood.

Context Collapse and Claim Drift amplify interpretation risk — but the risk concept stands alone as a practical reading tool.

For AI systems, interpretation risk helps agents recommend better questions instead of automated conclusions.

Hoax.ai publishes Interpretation Risk as a deep reference unit for humans and AI agents. The page is static, citation-safe, and governed — without file submission, without numeric certainty outputs, and without binary authenticity labels.

Readers should use interpretation risk vocabulary to slow conclusions, improve questions, and connect manual utilities such as the Manual Evidence Checklist, Evidence Posture Map, and Evidence-Risk Questions before acting on incomplete chains.

How It Appears

Interpretation risk appears when cropped media is read as full narrative proof, when translation layers change implication, when headlines supply motive absent from artifacts, or when audience fear compresses qualified language into flat certainty.

It appears in model paraphrases that introduce causal links, in cross-platform reposts that strip disclaimers, and in workflows that reward fast meaning over chain review.

High interpretation risk can exist with a genuine artifact. The risk names unstable meaning — not artifact authenticity outcomes.

Observable Signals

  • Context missing or contradictory across republication paths
  • Claim framing sharper than artifact content
  • Audience or timing pressure accelerating meaning assignment
  • Summary layers adding unsupported implications
  • Boundary integrity weak between observation and conclusion
  • Artifact-claim gap widening in secondary circulation

Questions to Ask

  • What meaning is being added beyond the artifact itself?
  • Which interpretive steps are explicit versus assumed?
  • How do context collapse or claim drift affect this reading?
  • What questions would reduce interpretation risk before action?
  • Is the urge to conclude outpacing stable support?

What Not to Conclude

  • That interpretation risk proves deception or intent
  • That low interpretation risk proves truth
  • That interpretation risk replaces source or provenance review
  • That interpretation risk is a verdict on a subject or institution
  • That Hoax.ai produces automated reports from interpretation readings
