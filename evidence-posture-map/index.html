Evidence Posture Map

This map shows how evidence posture is read as a sequence of structural questions — manually and conceptually — without issuing a verdict, score, or binary authenticity label.

Posture Flow Map

Evidence layers: Source, Provenance, Context, Traceability, Claim Drift, Boundary Integrity, Evidence Posture.

Source → Provenance → Context → Traceability → Claim Drift → Boundary Integrity → Evidence Posture

What Evidence Posture Means

Evidence posture names the observable condition of an evidence artifact before belief, action, publication, or escalation. It describes structure — source clarity, provenance strength, contextual fit, traceability, and claim alignment — not a final judgment about truth.

Why Posture Is Not a Verdict

Posture vocabulary slows premature conclusion. It replaces rushed true/false framing with bounded description of what the evidence layer can support. A weak posture does not mean “fake.” A strong structural reading does not mean “true.”

How the Map Should Be Read

Move left to right. Each step asks whether the evidence chain still holds at that layer. If a layer fails, later layers cannot compensate by narrative pressure. Use the Manual Evidence Checklist to work through questions at each step.

Where Evidence Risk Appears

Risk signals appear when source is unknown, provenance breaks, context collapses, traceability disappears, claims drift beyond artifacts, or boundaries between artifact and subject erode. Each signal is structural — not an accusation.

Weak Evidence and Strong Evidence

Why weak evidence does not automatically mean fake: Missing provenance or context often means uncertainty, not fabrication proof. Disciplined readers describe limits instead of inventing verdict language.

Why strong evidence does not automatically mean true: Coherent structure reduces interpretive risk but does not certify factual correctness. Posture describes condition, not cosmic truth.
